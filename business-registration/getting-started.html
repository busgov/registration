Proprietary (private) company

Proprietary companies are commonly known as private companies. They:

  • can have up to 50 non-employee shareholders
  • can raise funds from existing shareholders and employees
  • can't be listed on a share market
  • generally aren't allowed to raise money from the public by selling shares (like public companies can).

Proprietary companies must have at least:

  • one shareholder (also known as a member), and
  • one director (who is responsible for running the business, and for business debts in some cases).

You can be both. You can also be the sole shareholder and director (known as a one person company).

If you're not sure whether a private or public company structure best suits your business, consider getting advice from an independent professional.

Public company

A public company can:

  • have an unlimited number of non-employee shareholders
  • raise funds by selling shares to the general public
  • be listed on a share market (this means members of the public can buy shares to invest in your company) if it meets requirements.

A public company must have at least:

  • one shareholder
  • one secretary, and
  • three directors (who are responsible for running the business, and for business debts in some cases).

If you're not sure whether a private or public company structure best suits your business, consider getting advice from an independent professional.

Limited by shares

When a company is 'limited by shares', the shareholders are only responsible for company losses up to the value of their shares.

This is the most commonly used public company structure in Australia.

Limited by guarantee

This type of public company is often used by non-profit or charitable organisations.

When individuals become members of this type of company, they must agree to contribute an amount if the company is wound up.

No liability company

No liability companies can only undertake activities for mining purposes. This must be stated in the company's constitution.

Company name

All companies must choose a company name as part of the company registration process.

If you've reserved your company name already with the Australian Securities & Investments Commission (ASIC), choose the 'Use a reserved name' option.

What is a company name?

A company name is a company's legal name. This is the name that appears on all official documents and legal papers, such as legal contracts and registrations and property leases.

A company name has two parts:

  • The first part is a name that you choose (we'll check that it's available to register).
  • The second part identifies the company type (such as proprietary limited).
For example: Fishing Supplies Proprietary Limited

If you don't want to choose a name, you can use the company's Australian Company Number (ACN) as the first part of your company name. An ACN is a unique 9 digit number that's issued to your company when it's registered. On company registration certificates and the company register, names that use the ACN will display with 'A.C.N.' at the front.

For example, A.C.N. 123 456 789 Proprietary Limited

Do I also need to register a business name?

If you want to run a business under a name that's different to the company name, you'll need to register that name as a business name.

For example: Jane registers a company with the company name 'Farming Partners Pty Ltd'.

If Jane trades under this exact name (she advertises her company using this name, uses it for her shop signage, etc) then she doesn't need to register a business name.

If she wants to trade under:

  • the name 'Fresh Food', she must register 'Fresh Food' as a business name
  • 'Farming Partners' (without the Pty Ltd), she must register 'Farming Partners' as a business name.

Find out more about registering a business name on the ASIC website.

An ACN is a unique 9 digit number that's issued to your company when it's successfully registered. You still need to include a legal element to identify the company type. On company registration certificates and the company register, names that use the ACN will display with 'A.C.N.' at the front.

Cash versus Accrual accounting method

Cash accounting method

Cash accounting tracks the actual money coming in and out of your business.

Example of cash accounting
If you send an invoice on Tuesday, and receive the payment in your account on Thursday, you record the income against Thursday's date in your books.

You're using cash accounting if you account for GST on a sale when you receive payment (not when you issue the invoice).

You can choose the cash accounting method for GST if:

  • your annual turnover is less than $2 million and you’re a small business
  • you're accounting for your income using the cash method for income tax purposes.

If you don't meet any of the criteria above and want to use cash accounting for GST, this may be possible. Contact the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) to seek permission.

Accrual accounting method

If you use accrual accounting, you record expenses and sales when they take place, instead of when cash changes hands.

Example of accrual accounting
If you're a builder and have sent an invoice for a project you've completed, you record the sale in your books even though you haven't received payment yet.

You're using an accruals or non-cash accounting method if you account for GST on a sale when you issue an invoice or receive any part of the payment - whichever happens first.

Limited partners don't play any role in the day-to-day management of the business, and their liability for debt is limited to the amount they contributed to the partnership.

You must register a limited partnership in your state or territory. Contact your state or territory government to find out more.

A company is a separate legal entity from you, and has at least one shareholder (owner) and one director (who runs the business). Income and losses belong to the company. Companies can employ staff.

Companies

A company structure has higher costs and is more difficult to set up. Running a company can also be more expensive, because you have to meet reporting and other requirements under Australian company law. This is why the structure is generally considered to be better suited to medium to large businesses.

A company structure means that your business is a separate legal entity from you. This means the company has the same rights as a natural person and can have debt, sue and be sued. A company is owned by its shareholders and run by its directors.

The personal assets of directors and shareholders are separate from the company, so they can't be used to pay company debts. In certain cases where directors breach their duties, they may be held personally responsible for company losses.

The income and losses that the business makes belongs to the company, and is reported on a company tax return. The company tax rate applies to the business income.

If the company employs staff, the company is responsible for giving them their minimum employee entitlements and conditions (such as superannuation and leave).

Companies must appoint a public officer and let the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) know. Public officers are responsible for making sure the company complies with tax law, keeping tax records and submitting company tax returns. The ATO needs to know their details so they can contact them about company tax matters.

Proprietary limited company

Companies can be proprietary (private) or public. Most companies in Australia are proprietary limited. A proprietary limited company generally isn't allowed to raise money from the public by selling shares (like public companies can).

They must have:

  • no more than 50 non-employee shareholders
  • at least one shareholder (also known as a member)
  • at least one director.

You can be both a shareholder and a director. You can also be the sole shareholder and director.

When companies are 'limited', the liability of shareholders is limited to any unpaid value (if any) on their shares. This means that shareholders can only be responsible for company losses up to any amount unpaid on their shares.

Visit the Australian Securities & Commissions (ASIC) website to find out more about:

  • shareholders (members)
  • company officeholders (directors and secretaries)
  • duties of company officeholders.

Companies have to lodge an annual company tax return, which shows the company's income, deductions and income tax it is liable to pay.

Public companies

Unlike private companies, public companies can have an unlimited number of non-employee shareholders and raise money from the public by selling shares. A public company must have at least one shareholder, one secretary, and three directors.

What is GST?

GST is a tax of 10% on most goods and services sold in Australia. The money raised by GST is given to the state and territory governments, to provide public services to their communities.

Businesses collect GST on the goods and services they sell and forward it to the Australian Taxation Office (ATO).

  • put aside the GST you've collected
  • get tax invoices and claim GST credits for goods and services you buy for your own business
  • lodge activity statements or annual returns to the ATO
  • pay GST to the ATO (or get a GST refund).

  • letting ASIC know about changes to company details (such as changes to the registered office address, secretaries or directors)
  • lodging the company's financial reports
  • ensuring the company meets its legal requirements.

To be a secretary, a person must:

  • have agreed in writing to be a company secretary
  • be at least 18 years of age.

A secretary's appointment is effective on the day the company is successfully registered.

Find out more about the duties of officeholders (directors and secretaries) on the Australian Securities & Investments Commission (ASIC) website.

Who can't be a secretary?

Unless a court allows it, a person must not be a secretary if they:

  • are bankrupt
  • are in debt and have an agreement with creditors to manage the debt
  • have been convicted of offences such as fraud or offences under the Corporations Act 2001.

A company is not for profit if it:

  • doesn't pay dividends to its members

Government control

A government controls a company or organisation if the government controls it through either:

  • majority ownership of voting shares
  • specific legislation, or
  • the right to appoint and remove most 'directors' of the controlling body of the company or organisation.

Because of this control, a government agency can influence the operations of a company or organisation.

A company or organisation isn't controlled by a government simply because it receives most of its funding from a government.

Governments at all levels (Commonwealth, state, territory and local) can control a company or organisation.

Family and other partnerships

There is no legal paperwork required to form a family or other partnership. However, there are legal responsibilities that come with being a partner.

Each partner must report their share of the income on their own individual tax return, and pay tax at their individual income tax rate.

The partnership must also lodge a partnership income tax return every year with the Australian Taxation Office (ATO).

All partners share legal responsibility and any debt equally.

If your business employs staff, all partners are responsible for giving staff their minimum employee entitlements and conditions (such as superannuation and leave).

Limited partnerships

Partners in a limited partnership must register the partnership and comply with their relevant state or territory partnership legislation. This legislation is administered by the Consumer affairs or Fair trading office in each state and territory.

A limited partnership is considered a company for tax purposes and the partnership must lodge a company tax return with the Australian Taxation Office (ATO).

Not all partners share legal responsibility for the business. The limited partners only share responsibility and debt according to the limitations set out in the state or territory registration.

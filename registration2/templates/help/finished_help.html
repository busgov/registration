Australian Business Number (ABN)

If you run a business in Australia, you should have an ABN.

What is an ABN?

An ABN is a unique 9 digit number that identifies your business to the public, the Tax Office, and other government agencies.

Why should I have an ABN?

Having an ABN means you can:

  • confirm your business identity to others when ordering and invoicing
  • avoid having business clients withhold 46.5% of any payments they make to you
  • register a business name
  • register an Australian domain name.

You also need to have an ABN if you register for:

  • Goods and Services Tax (GST)
  • Pay As You Go (PAYG) withholding
  • Fuel Tax Credits (FTC)
  • Wine Equalisation Tax (WET)
  • Luxury Car Tax (LCT)

Goods & Services Tax (GST)

You should register for GST because in Step 4: Business activity you indicated your business plans to:

  • have turnover of $75,000 or more in your first year
  • charge passengers for taxi services
  • charge passengers for limousine travel

You should register for GST if you’ve just started a business and expect to have business turnover of $75,000 or more in your first year.

You must register for GST, no matter what your turnover is, if:

  • your business charges passengers for taxi services or limousine travel
  • you register for Wine Equalisation Tax (WET), Fuel Tax Credits (FTC) or Luxury Car Tax (LCT).

Once you register for GST, you must stay registered for at least 12 months.

What is GST?

GST is a tax of 10% on most goods and services sold in Australia. The money raised by GST is given to the state and territory governments, to provide public services to their communities.

Businesses collect GST on the goods and services they sell and forward it to the Tax Office.

What do I need to do?

If you’re registered for GST, you’ll need to:

  • work out which goods and services you sell are subject to GST
  • include GST in the prices you charge for those goods and services
  • give tax invoices to your customers that include the GST amount
  • account for GST in your books
  • put aside the GST you’ve collected
  • get tax invoices and claim GST credits for goods and services you buy for your own business
  • lodge activity statements or annual returns to the Tax Office
  • pay GST to the Tax Office (or get a GST refund).

PAYG Withholding

You should register for PAYG Withholding because in Step 3: Employees you indicated you plan to have employees.

What is PAYG Withholding?

When you have employees, you must withhold tax amounts from the wage or salary payments you make to employees, and forward these tax amounts to the Tax Office.

PAYG Withholding isn't a tax on your business - it's a tax on your employees' income that you help the Tax Office to collect.

What do I need to do?

Being registered for PAYG Withholding means you:

  • work out how much you need to withhold
  • report PAYG Withholding amounts to the Tax Office
  • pay PAYG Withholding amounts to the Tax Office when they're due
  • include PAYG Withholding amounts in your employees' pay slips and payment summaries.
Find out more

Read about PAYG Withholding on the Tax Office website.

Fringe Benefits Tax (FBT)

You should consider registering for FBT because in Step 3: Employees you indicated you plan to give fringe benefits to your employees.

What is FBT?

As an employer, if you give certain fringe benefits to your employees, you must pay tax on the value of the fringe benefits.

What do I need to do?

Being registered for FBT means you:

  • work out how much FBT you have to pay
  • keep records related to the fringe benefits you provide
  • report fringe benefits on your employees' payment summaries
  • report fringe benefits in the Tax Ofice
  • pay the FBT to the Tax Office.

Find out more

Read about FBT on the Tax Office website.

Luxury Car Tax (LCT)

You should register for LCT because in Step 4: Business activity you indicated you plan to sell or import luxury cars.

Having a turnover of $75,000 or more means you should register for GST.

If you're registered for GST and you sell or import luxury cars, then you need to register for and pay LCT.

What do I need to do?

Being registered for LCT means you:

  • work out the LCT payable and include it in the import or sale
  • account for LCT sales and imports in your books
  • report and pay LCT to the Tax Office.
Find out more

Read about LCT on the Tax Office website.

Fuel Tax Credits

You should consider registering for FTC because in Step 4: Business activity you indicated you plan to use taxable fuels in your business.

What is FTC?

If you register for FTC, you can claim credits for the fuel tax you pay on these fuels. The amount of fuel tax credits you can claim depends on the type of fuel you use and what business activity you use it in.

What do I need to do?

Being registered for FTC means you:

  • work out how much you can claim on eligible fuels
  • keep records of the type and quantity of fuels, when you bought the fuels and how you used them in your business.

Find out more

Read about FTC on the Tax Office website.

Wine Equalisation Tax (WET)

You should consider registering for WET because in Step 4: Business activity you indicated you plan to import or produce wines, or sell wines wholesale.

Having a turnover of $75,000 or more means you should register for GST.

You must register for WET if:

  • you're registered for GST and
  • you import or produce wine or sell wines wholesale, where the wines are subject to WET.
What is WET?

WET is a tax on the consumption of wine in Australia, and it's usually applied to the last wholesale sale - this is usually the sale to the retailer. You collect the tax to give to the Tax Office.

What do I need to do?

Being registered for WET means you:

  • work out which sales are subject to WET
  • issue tax invoices that show the WET amount
  • claim any WET credits you may be entitled to (including rebates or refunds)
  • account for WET amounts in your books
  • put aside WET you've collected
  • report WET to the Tax Office.
Find out more

Read about WET on the Tax Office website.
